Background

Love: The Dominant Theme
of Popular Music

Music is a huge part of what influences us and our culture — it encodes and reproduces the ideologies of its time. Among all themes in mainstream popular songs, romantic love has remained the most dominant and persistent across decades. Empirical content analyses of chart-topping songs consistently confirm that love appears in the majority of lyrical content: Ruth (2019) found love in 57% of German popular songs, and Christenson et al. (2019) reported that 67.3% of US top-40 songs referenced relationships and love.

The expression of love in songs, however, is not static. Studies reveal a diachronic decline in romantic uses of the word "love," a rise in sexually explicit and lust-oriented themes, and a progressive change from idealized romance toward more complicated and darker emotional articulations (Smiler et al., 2017; Madanikia & Bartholomew, 2014).

This study addresses that gap by combining corpus linguistics tools with Halliday's Systemic Functional Linguistics (SFL) to trace how the lexicogrammar of love in popular lyrics has changed from the 1980s through the 2020s.

Research Questions

01

What are the most frequent lexical items associated with the expression of love in popular song lyrics across the five decades?

02

What collocational and concordance patterns emerge from the selected lexical items across the designated decades?

03

How do the changing stylistic choices reflect or challenge broader sociocultural ideologies surrounding modern romance, individual autonomy, and emotional vulnerability?

Theoretical Framework

Halliday's Systemic Functional
Linguistics (SFL)

SFL posits that language is organized around three simultaneous metafunctions. In this study, each metafunction maps directly to how corpus data is interpreted — frequency reveals ideational content, collocation exposes interpersonal positioning, and concordance structures show textual organization.

Ideational Metafunction

Field

Represents experience through transitivity — processes, participants, and circumstances.

"What love is" — decoded through frequency lists.

Interpersonal Metafunction

Tenor

Enacts social relationships through mood and modality between participants.

"How love is enacted" — decoded through collocation patterns.

Textual Metafunction

Mode

Organizes information through theme–rheme structures and information flow.

"How love is presented" — decoded through KWIC concordance.

Methodology

Building the Corpus

A comparable, diachronic corpus totaling approximately 50,000 words, purposefully sampled from pop love songs across five temporal milestones and processed through AntConc for rigorous linguistic analysis.

Corpus Composition
  • ~10,000 words per sub-corpus
  • 30–40 songs per decade
  • Pop genre only for register consistency
  • Romantic, lustful, and longing themes
Data Sources & Tools
  • Genius & AZLyrics (verified repositories)
  • AntConc for frequency, collocation & KWIC
  • UTF-8 plain text (.txt) files
  • Log-likelihood statistics for collocation strength

Analysis Pipeline

Stage 1 — Sampling & Collection

Corpus Construction

Five equal sub-corpora (1980s–2020s) of ~10,000 words each (30–40 pop love songs per decade) were assembled from Genius and AZLyrics, covering only pop songs with romantic, lustful, or longing themes.

Stage 2 — Cleaning & Normalization

Text Preprocessing

Systematic removal of structural tags ([Chorus], [Verse 1], [Bridge]), artist names, and credits. Repetitive ad-libs, non-lexical vocables ("oh," "yeah," "la-la-la"), and all punctuation were stripped out to prevent skewed frequency distributions.

Stage 3 — Processing & Analysis

AntConc Analysis

Analysis using AntConc proceeded in three stages: (1) frequency lists per sub-corpus to identify prominent love-related lexical items per decade; (2) collocation analysis for top node words; and (3) qualitative KWIC concordance examination through an SFL lens to interpret ideational, interpersonal, and textual meanings.

1980s

Idealized Romance

💌

Love is construed as a stable, eternal, internal state — a shared cultural script of permanence and mutual commitment consistent with Wilkinson's (1976) observation that mid-century songs allowed emotional expression within a framework of lasting devotion.

Top Love-Related Lexical Items

SFL Interpretation

Dominated by mental processes of affection and perception (ideational metafunction). The prevalence of forever and true shows an ideology of romantic permanence. Collocates of the pronoun I include nothing (likelihood = 156.95) and my (likelihood = 43.00), constructing a knowing, feeling subject who narrates love through high declarative certainty and low modality.

Collocational Note

Collocates of know include at (likelihood = 89.95), producing concordance patterns of declarative epistemic certainty about emotional states.

Sample Concordance Lines

"I'll love you more than I can say."

"I wanna know what love is."

Dominant metaphor: LOVE IS AN ENDURING STATE — a stable emotional reality the speaker narrates, not an event they undergo.

1990s

Relational & Dynamic

🌹

The 1990s sees a peak in the raw frequency of "love" (305) alongside the emergence of relational and dynamic vocabulary. Love shifts from something the singer possesses to something that happens to the singer.

Top Love-Related Lexical Items

love305
baby108
fall40

SFL Interpretation

A shift from mental to material/relational processes — love as something that happens to the singer. The rise of baby as the second most frequent item aligns with Climent & Coll-Florit's (2020) finding that "baby" is the most recurrent metaphorical expression for the love object. The strongest collocate of want is baby (likelihood = 234.38), alongside matters (likelihood = 125.89) and mean (likelihood = 54.40).

Key SFL Shift

Temporal and contingent framing introduces modality and comparison as interpersonal resources — "love takes time," "love is like losing."

Sample Concordance Lines

"Love takes time to heal."

"I'm gonna love you like I'm gonna lose you."

Dominant shift: LOVE IS A PROCESS — love now occurs over time and involves contingency, making the speaker a participant in an event rather than the holder of a state.

2000s

Fragmentation & Compulsion

💊

The romantic lexicon fragments: "love" drops sharply while novel items (halo, bleeding) reflect increasing negativity, personalization, and spatial metaphor. The "addiction" frame emerges — love as involuntary dependency.

Top Love-Related Lexical Items

love134
baby100
halo67
belong34
girl33
waiting29
bleeding27

SFL Interpretation

Expansion into visual/spatial metaphors (halo, belong) and affective extremity (bleeding). The collocational profile of I is dominated by wanna (likelihood = 109.09), halo (likelihood = 53.49), fallin' (likelihood = 42.70), and bleeding (likelihood = 27.55). Two critical SFL observations emerge: (1) fallin' shows a processual construal — love is an event rather than a state; (2) the addiction metaphor reframes love as involuntary physiological dependency (Kövecses, 2014).

Temporal Stasis

The item waiting introduces temporal stasis absent from earlier decades — a change toward lyrical themes of longing and deferred gratification (Fell et al., 2019).

Sample Concordance Lines

"I think I'm fallin' for you."

"I'm addicted to your love, baby."

Dominant metaphor: LOVE IS AN ADDICTION — love is construed as a compulsion, removing agency from the romantic subject entirely.

2010s

Immediacy & Possession

🔥

Dramatic compression of the love lexicon: "love" falls to just 51 occurrences. The most distinctive items construe love through immediacy, physicality, and possession rather than through enduring emotion. Abstract words like forever and true begin their exit.

Top Love-Related Lexical Items

love51
edge39
kiss17
tonight17
mine16
mind14

SFL Interpretation

The ideational content shifts from mental processes (feeling, knowing) toward material processes (kissing, possessing) and from circumstantial elements of duration (forever, always) toward temporal immediacy (tonight). Prominent collocates include until (likelihood = 38.94), mine (likelihood = 29.56), and move (likelihood = 26.42). The high frequency of mine signals LOVE IS POSSESSION as a dominant cultural metaphor (Climent & Coll-Florit, 2020).

Lust Themes Rising

Consistent with Madanikia & Bartholomew (2014): significant increase in lust themes accompanying the sharp decline of the word "love."

Sample Concordance Lines

"You know that I want to be with you all the time / Until I make you mine."

Dominant metaphor: LOVE IS POSSESSION — the beloved is reconstituted as an object to be obtained. The possessive pronoun mine encodes an interpersonal dynamic of ownership.

2020s

Concreteness & Disruption

🌙

Partial recovery of "love" (89), but a spare emotional lexicon. Abstract words like forever and true have vanished entirely. Love is either an effect that can be produced or naturalized via cosmic metaphor. A double movement of commodification and cosmic nostalgia.

Top Love-Related Lexical Items

love89
like83
baby50
want44

fall29
mind26

SFL Interpretation

Collocations marked by novelty and disruption: supernatural (likelihood = 40.58), doesn't (likelihood = 39.25), butter (likelihood = 26.43), and dynamite (likelihood = 19.18). A double movement emerges: love as a produced effect ("make you fall in love" — material process with explicit Agent and Goal) and love naturalized via cosmic metaphor ("moon needs the stars"), reclaiming experiential proximity identified by Climent & Coll-Florit (2020) as the most enduring love metaphor across seven decades.

Lexical Attrition

Feel and heart — top emotional items in the 1980s and 1990s — no longer appear prominently. Forever and true have vanished entirely, consistent with Wang et al. (2025).

Sample Concordance Lines

"You make me wanna make you fall in love."

"Like the moon needs the stars."

Dominant shift: LOVE IS TRANSACTIONAL — construed as an outcome to be engineered. Love is also LOVE IS COSMIC NATURE — a simultaneous nostalgic reach for permanence.

Sociocultural Analysis

From Communal Script to
Individualized Affect

The diachronic trajectory of the love lexicon — from forever, true, heart in the 1980s to mine, tonight, edge in the 2010s to supernatural, butter, dynamite in the 2020s — shows a decisive shift in the field of discourse.

In SFL terms, this is a shift in the field of discourse: the dominant ideational representation of love has moved from a shared cultural script of permanence and mutuality to a private and contingent transactional experience. This finding resonates with Illouz's sociological argument that modern romance has been restructured by the logic of consumption and choice — where love is evaluated rather than simply experienced — a logic that the 2020s concordance of "make you fall in love" makes linguistically explicit (Fuentes & Peregrín, 2021).

Conclusion

The Evolution of Love's Language

A systematic diachronic transformation across five decades, traced through corpus frequency, collocational statistics, and KWIC concordance lines interpreted through an SFL lens.

Ideational Representation

From stable mental states (forever, true, heart) to material processes and possessive relations (mine, tonight, kiss). What love is has changed fundamentally.

Interpersonal Tenor

From declarative devotion ("more than I can say") to negotiated demand ("make you mine," "doesn't"). How love is enacted has shifted from certainty to contingency.

Textual Organization

Now privileges immediacy and novelty over duration and convention — reproducing broader sociocultural ideologies of individualization and emotional commodification of romantic idealism.

Core Finding

A systematic transformation from stable mental states (1980s) to contingent, transactional, and commodified experiences (2020s).

The lexicogrammar of popular love songs has faithfully encoded — and reproduced — the broader sociological restructuring of intimacy by the logic of consumption and individual choice.

Recommendations for Future Research

  • Expand the corpus to include non-English and non-Western song traditions for cross-cultural comparison.
  • Incorporate multimodal analysis of music videos to examine how visual semiotics interact with lyrical construals of love.
  • Apply longitudinal SFL clause-level transitivity analysis to full texts rather than keyword samples, deepening granularity of ideological tracing.
